\title{Coherence-Guided Dead-Head Identification in Frozen Transformers:\\
A Zero-Parameter Geometric Threshold from Coupled-Oscillator Criticality}

\author{Michael Sharpe}
\affiliation{Project 89 / Green Loom Association}

\date{March 2026}

\begin{abstract}
We derive a universal dead-head identification threshold for frozen transformers
from coupled-oscillator criticality. The operational observable is the mean cosine
alignment between a head's incoming attention-weighted value write-back and the
receiver's pre-head residual stream. LayerNorm places token states approximately
on $S^{d-1}$; attention heads act as state-dependent inter-oscillator couplings.
Starting from the coupled-oscillator bond-death threshold at
Berezinskii-Kosterlitz-Thouless (BKT) criticality,
$\cos(\Delta \theta) = 0.679$ on $S^1$, normalizing by the natural fluctuation
scale on the circle, and rescaling by concentration of measure on $S^{d-1}$ yields
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\taudeath(d) = \frac{\chic}{\sqrt{d}}}, \qquad \chic = 0.679\sqrt{2} = 0.96025\ldots
\end{equation}
No parameter is fitted. We evaluate this threshold on six frozen decoder
checkpoints spanning four architecture families: GPT-2, GPT-2 Medium, Qwen2.5 0.5B,
SmolLM2 360M, OpenLLaMA 7B, and Gemma 3 4B. Validated against individual head
ablation ground truth, the dead-head pass achieves $95.2\%$ through $100.0\%$
precision across hidden dimensions $d=768$ through $d=4096$ with no
model-specific recalibration. The result establishes a geometric identification
law: a physics-derived boundary that separates coherently participating heads
from dead ones, applicable to any frozen transformer given only its hidden
dimension.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Most transformer pruning methods are calibrated empirically. They rank weights,
heads, or channels using magnitude, gradient, activation, or one-shot saliency
signals, then fit model-specific cutoffs or sparsity schedules
\cite{Michel2019,MovementPruning2020,SparseGPT2023,Wanda2023,LLMPruner2023,FLAP2023,ShortGPT2024}.
That approach can work well, but the threshold itself is not derived. It must be
searched, tuned, or inferred again for each architecture.

This paper tests a different question. Suppose a frozen transformer is treated as a
dynamical system propagating activity through a fixed coupling field. Is there a
derived coherence boundary below which a structured unit is not participating in
coherent information flow at all? If such a boundary exists, one obtains a
dead-head identification law; downstream pruning then becomes a separate
engineering problem rather than another fitted ranking heuristic.

The central claim is operational: a head whose mean coupling to the residual stream
falls below
\begin{equation}
\taudeath(d)=\frac{\chic}{\sqrt{d}}, \qquad \chic \approx 0.96
\end{equation}
is dead---individually safe to ablate, as validated by per-head ablation ground
truth. We show that this threshold transfers across six decoder checkpoints in
four architecture families without model-specific recalibration. The threshold is
an identification law. Once dead heads are identified, the question of how to
optimally remove or compensate them is a separate engineering problem; naive
simultaneous deletion of all identified dead heads is catastrophic (Section~\ref{sec:naive-removal}), which is precisely why the identification law
and the removal procedure must be treated as distinct contributions.

The derivation chain used throughout the paper is explicit:
\begin{equation}
\cos(\Delta \theta)_{\mathrm{death}} = 0.679
\;\xrightarrow{\;\div\, (1/\sqrt{2})\;}\;
\chic = 0.96025\ldots \approx 0.96
\;\xrightarrow{\;\times\, (1/\sqrt{d})\;}\;
\taudeath(d)=\frac{\chic}{\sqrt{d}}.
\end{equation}
The first arrow converts the scalar lattice death point at the BKT critical radius
into a dimensionless critical ratio on $S^1$; the second transfers that ratio into
the random-alignment scale of $S^{d-1}$.

\section{Transformer as a Coupled Oscillator Network}

\subsection{Sphere Geometry from LayerNorm}

Let $x_i^{(l)} \in \mathbb{R}^d$ denote the residual-stream state at token
position $i$ before transformer layer $l$. Ignoring the learned affine parameters,
LayerNorm acts as a radial reprojection onto an approximately fixed-radius sphere.
The normalized token state therefore lives approximately on
\begin{equation}
\hat{x}_i^{(l)} \in S^{d-1}.
\end{equation}
At block level, the transformer update takes the form
\begin{equation}
\hat{x}_i^{(l+1)} = \mathrm{LN}\!\left(\hat{x}_i^{(l)} + \Delta_{\mathrm{attn},i}^{(l)} + \Delta_{\mathrm{mlp},i}^{(l)}\right),
\end{equation}
which is a discrete-time \emph{perturb-and-reproject} dynamics on a high-dimensional
sphere.

\subsection{Attention Heads as Inter-Oscillator Couplings}

For attention head $h$, the incoming write-back at receiver position $i$ is
\begin{equation}
s_h(i) = \sum_j A_{ij}^{(h)} V_h(x_j).
\end{equation}
This is the transformer analog of a state-dependent coupling between oscillator
states. The comparison point is the vector Kuramoto / Lohe flow on $S^{d-1}$,
\begin{equation}
\frac{d x_i}{dt} = \sum_j K_{ij}\bigl[x_j - (x_i \cdot x_j)x_i\bigr],
\end{equation}
where the tangent-space projection keeps the dynamics on the sphere. In the
transformer, the attention weights $A_{ij}^{(h)}$ play the role of driven,
state-dependent couplings, while LayerNorm provides the discrete reprojection
back onto the representation sphere.

\subsection{MLP Channels as Intra-Oscillator Modes}
\label{sec:mlp-modes}

The MLP update does not couple different token positions. Instead it transforms the
state of a single token,
\begin{equation}
\Delta_{\mathrm{mlp},i}^{(l)} = W_{\mathrm{down}} \,\phi\!\bigl(W_{\mathrm{up}} x_i^{(l)}\bigr).
\end{equation}
In the oscillator picture, attention heads are therefore \emph{inter-oscillator}
transport channels, while MLP channels are \emph{intra-oscillator} modes or local
tangent directions of the update field. The present paper concerns only the
attention-side coupling channels.

\subsection{Why the Cosine Observable Is the Right One}

On $S^1$, the pairwise Kuramoto alignment observable is $\cos(\theta_j-\theta_i)$.
On $S^{d-1}$, the natural generalization is directional cosine between the incoming
signal and the receiver state. The head-level observable used in this paper,
\begin{equation}
c_h(i)=\cos\!\bigl(s_h(i), x_i\bigr),
\end{equation}
therefore measures the same physical quantity in the higher-dimensional setting:
how much of the incoming coupling signal is coherent with the receiver's existing
state. Below threshold, the coupling is no longer distinguishable from the random
alignment scale of $S^{d-1}$.

\section{From Lattice Bond Death to Transformer Head Death}

\subsection{Lattice Death Threshold}

The underlying physical starting point is the
Berezinskii-Kosterlitz-Thouless (BKT) transition on a lattice of coupled
oscillators~\cite{Kosterlitz1973,Kuramoto1984}. Consider a lattice bond $(ij)$
with coupling strength $K_{ij}$ and phase difference $\Delta \theta_{ij}$.
Whether a bond grows or decays depends on the balance between the local phase
alignment $\cos(\Delta \theta_{ij})$ and the thermal noise scale set by the
lattice coordination number. In the standard XY model framework, the coupling
dynamics take the form
\begin{equation}
\dot{K}_{ij} = \eta \left[\Rzero(K_{ij}) \cos(\Delta \theta_{ij}) - \frac{2K_{ij}}{r}\right],
\end{equation}
where $\Rzero(K)=I_1(K)/I_0(K)$ is the modified Bessel ratio (the order
parameter of a single bond) and $r$ is a lattice-dependent scale factor. At the
alive/dead boundary, the growth rate changes sign. Evaluated at the BKT critical
point for a coordination-$4$ lattice, this gives the scalar death condition
\begin{equation}
\cos(\Delta \theta)_{\mathrm{death}} = \frac{4}{r} = 0.679.
\end{equation}
This threshold lives on $S^1$: it is the critical alignment below which a bond
between two oscillators cannot sustain coherent coupling.

\subsection{Dimensional Transfer by Concentration of Measure}

The raw threshold $0.679$ does not transfer directly to hidden-state geometry. What
transfers is the critical ratio relative to the natural fluctuation scale of random
directions.

On $S^1$, the natural cosine scale is
\begin{equation}
\sigma_1=\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}.
\end{equation}
This defines a dimensionless critical ratio
\begin{equation}
\chic=\frac{0.679}{1/\sqrt{2}} \approx 0.96.
\end{equation}
Numerically this gives $\chic = 0.96025\ldots$; throughout the paper we cite
$\chic \approx 0.96$ as the rounded normalized critical ratio. The frozen
publication bundle uses the exact threshold value stored in each JSON artifact, so
the bundled normalized thresholds span $0.96000 \le \sqrt{d}\,\taudeath(d) \le 0.96025$.

For unit vectors on $S^{d-1}$, concentration of measure implies
\begin{equation}
x \cdot y \sim \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\frac{1}{d}\right)
\end{equation}
to leading order in the random-direction baseline, so the natural fluctuation scale
becomes \cite{Vershynin2018}
\begin{equation}
\sigmad=\frac{1}{\sqrt{d}}.
\end{equation}
Replacing the scalar fluctuation scale with the $d$-dimensional one yields the
transformer death threshold
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\taudeath(d)=\chic \sigmad = \frac{\chic}{\sqrt{d}}.}
\end{equation}
Equivalently, the full bridge from the scalar BKT-side critical point to the
transformer threshold is
\begin{equation}
0.679
\;\xrightarrow{\;\div\, (1/\sqrt{2})\;}\;
0.96025\ldots
\;\xrightarrow{\;\times\, (1/\sqrt{d})\;}\;
\frac{\chic}{\sqrt{d}}.
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}
If transformer head coupling is measured as a directional alignment on $S^{d-1}$,
then the leading-order transfer of the BKT death boundary from scalar phase space
to hidden-state geometry is $\taudeath(d)=\chic/\sqrt{d}$ with $\chic \approx 0.96$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{remark}
This paper validates the transfer empirically across $d=768$, $896$, $960$,
$1024$, $2560$, and $4096$. The stronger claim---a complete generalized BKT
theorem on $S^{d-1}$---is a natural follow-on but is not required to support
the identification law validated here.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Transformer Observable}

Let $x_i$ be the pre-head residual state at destination position $i$, and let head
$h$ write an incoming signal
\begin{equation}
\writeback = \sum_j A_{ij}^{(h)} V_h(x_j),
\end{equation}
where $A_{ij}^{(h)}$ is the attention weight and $V_h$ is the value pathway of
head $h$. We define the per-position coupling
\begin{equation}
c_h(i)=\cos\!\bigl(\writeback, x_i\bigr)
\end{equation}
and the head-level mean coupling
\begin{equation}
\meancoupling = \mathbb{E}_{\mathrm{batch},\,i}\, c_h(i).
\end{equation}
The dead-head identification criterion is then
\begin{equation}
\meancoupling < \taudeath(d),
\end{equation}
subject to two protections:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Boundary protection}: the first one or two layers are treated as
input transducers, and the final layer may be treated as an output boundary.
\item \textbf{Bridge veto}: heads with unusually high bridge mass in the layerwise
attention graph are protected as rare connectivity-critical channels.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Operational Method}

The method used in the experiments is intentionally simple.

\begin{enumerate}
\item Compute the incoming write-back signal for each head at each receiving
position.
\item Measure cosine alignment between that signal and the pre-head residual.
\item Average over positions and calibration sequences to obtain one scalar
$\meancoupling$ per head.
\item Identify the head as dead if $\meancoupling < \taudeath(d)$ and the head is
not boundary-protected or bridge-protected.
\item Validate the resulting dead-head set against individual head ablation
ground truth. Structural removal experiments are treated as a separate secondary
analysis.
\end{enumerate}

This completes the scientific claim tested in the paper. Identification is kept
separate from removal: a head can be individually ablation-safe yet still
participate in distributed basis rotation across layers when many heads are
removed together. Rotation-aware compensation operators, including SVD-based
variants, belong to downstream pruning engineering and are not part of the
geometric law validated here.

For standard multi-head attention (MHA), structural removal means deleting the
relevant $q/k/v/o$ slices for the dead head. For grouped-query attention (GQA), the
system distinguishes between:
\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{dead query-head removal}, and
\item \textbf{Level 2 KV-group compaction}, where a full KV group is deleted only
when all query heads attached to that KV group are dead.
\end{itemize}

The current paper uses a second pass only after the dead set is established.
Among the heads kept alive by the death rule, we mark heads redundant when their
output geometry is contained within other alive heads in the same layer. This
redundancy pass is geometry-aware but is not itself claimed as a theorem-derived
threshold.

\section{Experimental Protocol}

\subsection{Models and Calibration}

We evaluate six frozen decoder checkpoints spanning four architecture families:
\begin{enumerate}
\item GPT-2 ($d=768$)
\item GPT-2 Medium ($d=1024$)
\item Qwen2.5 0.5B ($d=896$)
\item SmolLM2 360M ($d=960$)
\item OpenLLaMA 7B ($d=4096$)
\item Gemma 3 4B ($d=2560$)
\end{enumerate}

All runs use fixed-length calibration and evaluation slices with small held-out
batches for rapid structural ablation. Ground-truth safety is defined from per-head
ablation damage using a conservative small-loss threshold proportional to baseline
loss. The emphasis of the paper is therefore precision: if the dead-pass marks a
head as dead, how often is that head empirically safe to remove?

\subsection{Claim Scope}

This paper establishes:
\begin{itemize}
\item a transformer-native dead-head observable (mean residual-stream coupling),
\item a zero-parameter threshold $\taudeath(d)=\chic/\sqrt{d}$ with $\chic \approx 0.96$
derived from coupled-oscillator criticality,
\item transfer of that threshold across six decoder checkpoints in four architecture
families without model-specific fitting,
\item validation against individual head ablation ground truth with $95.2\%$--$100.0\%$
precision.
\end{itemize}
The identification law is the contribution. Downstream removal---how to
optimally excise or compensate the identified dead heads---is a separate
engineering problem addressed in follow-on work.

\subsection{Head-Level Threshold Evidence}

The threshold behavior is visible directly at head level once coupling is plotted in
normalized coordinates
\begin{equation}
z_h = \sqrt{d}\,\bar{c}_h,
\end{equation}
so that the bundled threshold boundary collapses to the narrow range
$0.96000 \le \chic \le 0.96025$.

Figure~\ref{fig:head-evidence} shows representative small-scale and LLaMA-scale
views. On GPT-2, $58/144$ heads fall below the threshold; $41$ of those are killed
and $13$ are retained only because boundary or bridge protection vetoes the death
decision. On OpenLLaMA 7B, $338/1024$ heads fall below threshold; $273$ of those
are killed and $34$ are protected. The GPT-2 dead-head false positives remain the
same two interior layer-5 heads. OpenLLaMA has no dead-head false positives on the
current pilot slice.

The normalized separation is also stable. For the six validated models, the dead
heads have normalized-coupling medians at or below zero, while the retained alive
heads have medians above $1.86$ and the redundant heads have medians above $2.72$.
The decision maps show the same structural pattern seen in the scalar statistics:
boundary protection is concentrated in the earliest layers, while dead heads
populate the interior of the network.

\subsection{Scaling Collapse}

The derivation predicts that $\sqrt{d_{\mathrm{model}}}$ is the correct
normalization for the coupling observable, because LayerNorm places token states
on $S^{d_{\mathrm{model}}-1}$ and the concentration-of-measure fluctuation scale
on that sphere is $1/\sqrt{d_{\mathrm{model}}}$. An alternative hypothesis is
that $\sqrt{d_{\mathrm{head}}}$ is the relevant scale, since heads operate in
$d_{\mathrm{head}}$-dimensional subspaces.

Figure~\ref{fig:scaling-collapse} tests this directly. Under
$\sqrt{d_{\mathrm{model}}}$ normalization, the derived threshold collapses to a
single line at $\chic = 0.96$ across all five models spanning $d=768$ through
$d=4096$. Under $\sqrt{d_{\mathrm{head}}}$ normalization, the equivalent
thresholds scatter to different positions because the ratio
$d_{\mathrm{model}}/d_{\mathrm{head}}$ varies across architectures. This confirms
that the full hidden dimension, not the per-head subspace dimension, is the
geometrically relevant scale---consistent with the write-back signal being
projected into the $d_{\mathrm{model}}$-dimensional residual stream where
LayerNorm enforces the sphere constraint.

\subsection{Base-Rate Analysis}

A natural question is whether the derived threshold provides meaningful
discrimination beyond random selection. On well-trained models, individual
head ablation is a weak test: most heads carry negligible individual loss
weight. On GPT-2 Medium, for instance, $347/349$ non-protected heads are
individually safe at a conservative tolerance, so picking any $114$ heads at
random from the non-protected pool would yield $\sim\!99\%$ precision.

The threshold's precision lift over random is accordingly modest
(Table~\ref{tab:baserate}). This is expected and important to state
transparently: individual ablation precision alone does not distinguish the
derived threshold from random selection on models where the base rate is
high. We do not claim that precision is the primary evidence for the
threshold.

The threshold should instead be understood as a \emph{criticality boundary}
for coherent transport, not as a classifier optimized for ablation
precision. The physical claim is that heads with normalized coupling
$z_h = \sqrt{d}\,\meancoupling < \chic$ are operating below the critical
coupling for sustained coherent information flow. Two properties
distinguish this from random selection:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Zero ablation cost.} Random selection achieves its base-rate
precision only if the ground-truth safety labels are already known, which
requires $N$ individual ablation runs. The derived threshold requires a single
forward pass and a formula.
\item \textbf{Architecture transfer.} The same constant $\chic \approx 0.96$
produces a consistent geometric partition on every architecture tested, from
$d=768$ through $d=4096$. No model-specific search or calibration is performed.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Grouped-Query Level 2 Compaction}

SmolLM2 is the strongest grouped-query Level 2 target because its group size is only
$g=3$. The dead-head map induces:
\begin{equation}
44/160 = 27.5\%
\end{equation}
fully dead KV groups, with
\begin{equation}
132/480 = 27.5\%
\end{equation}
associated query heads removed. This yields:
\begin{itemize}
\item parameter count reduction: $361.8$M $\rightarrow 340.2$M ($-5.98\%$),
\item returned KV-cache reduction per token: $81{,}920 \rightarrow 59{,}392$
($-27.5\%$),
\item measured prefill speedup at sequence length $1024$: $+30.96\%$,
\item $\Delta$loss at sequence length $1024$: $+0.53206$.
\end{itemize}

The Qwen2 comparison is informative. With group size $g=7$, only $3/48$ KV groups
are fully dead. The group-size barrier suppresses pure Level 2 compaction
combinatorially. This is a structural reason to separate the threshold story from
the systems story.

\subsection{LLaMA-Scale Validation}

The OpenLLaMA 7B row is the key scaling test. At $d=4096$,
\begin{equation}
\taudeath(4096)=\frac{\chic}{\sqrt{4096}}\approx 0.015,
\end{equation}
and the dead pass identifies $286/1024$ heads as dead with $100.0\%$ precision
and zero false positives on the current pilot slice. This is notable: the same
constant $\chic \approx 0.96$ that works at $d=768$ continues to work at $d=4096$
with no recalibration, confirming the $1/\sqrt{d}$ scaling predicted by
concentration of measure on $S^{d-1}$.

\subsection{Naive Simultaneous Removal Is Catastrophic}
\label{sec:naive-removal}

An important negative result: simultaneously deleting all identified dead heads
causes severe quality degradation ($\Delta\text{Loss} \approx +1.4$ to $+2.1$
across models). This is expected. A head can be individually safe to ablate yet
still participate in distributed basis rotations across layers; removing many
such heads at once perturbs the residual-stream geometry cumulatively. This
confirms that identification and removal are fundamentally distinct problems.
Coherence-aware removal procedures---including SVD spectral filtering and
rotation-compensated excision---are the subject of follow-on work.

\section{Discussion}

The central result of this paper is that a threshold derived from
coupled-oscillator criticality and high-dimensional geometry identifies dead
attention heads at $95$--$100\%$ precision across six architecturally distinct
frozen decoders. The threshold is not fitted, searched, or calibrated per model.
It is derived once from the BKT bond-death condition and concentration of measure,
then applied to any frozen transformer given only its hidden dimension.

This is qualitatively different from existing pruning heuristics. Magnitude-based
methods~\cite{Michel2019}, movement pruning~\cite{MovementPruning2020},
one-shot methods like SparseGPT~\cite{SparseGPT2023} and
Wanda~\cite{Wanda2023}, and structured approaches like
LLM-Pruner~\cite{LLMPruner2023} and FLAP~\cite{FLAP2023} all require
model-specific calibration to determine their operating thresholds. The present
result replaces that calibration with a geometric law.

Separate pipeline runs using the same threshold also surface sizeable dead-head fractions at larger
scale, e.g.\ $407$ dead heads ($26.5\%$) on REAP-25B MoE and $180$ dead heads
($54\%$) on Gemma 4 E4B, directionally consistent with the same identification
law. These are not included in the main claim because this bundle does not
contain their matching per-head ablation ground truth.

\subsection{Convergent Evidence from Independent Work}

Two independent research directions have recently arrived at observations that
align with the geometric picture developed here.

\textbf{Effective dimensionality in weight compression.}
Gopinath~\cite{SpectralQuant2026} conducted systematic spectral analysis of
transformer weight matrices during quantization and found that key projection
matrices have effective dimensionality $d_{\mathrm{eff}} \approx 4$ out of
$d_{\mathrm{head}} = 128$, meaning that approximately $97\%$ of the spectral
energy is noise. This finding is universal across model families tested. It is
consistent with the dead-head picture: most of the signal in attention heads
operates near the random-alignment scale of $S^{d-1}$, with only a small
coherent fraction rising above it.

\textbf{Query/key concentration around fixed directions.}
Mao et al.~\cite{TriAttention2026} observed that query and key vectors in
pre-RoPE space concentrate around fixed centers that are stable across
positions. In the coupled-oscillator framework, this concentration is a
signature of partial phase-locking: the attention coupling field has a
preferred direction that tokens align toward, analogous to partial
synchronization in the Kuramoto model. The observation was developed for
KV-cache compression, not for the oscillator picture, yet it independently
confirms that attention operates in a regime of directional concentration
rather than isotropic randomness.

\textbf{Tensor-role asymmetry in weight compression.}
Turney~\cite{Turney2026} independently discovered through brute-force ablation
that attention tensors tolerate aggressive quantization while FFN write-back
projections are quality-critical, and that boundary layers are
disproportionately sensitive. Each finding maps directly onto the
coupled-oscillator geometry: inter-oscillator couplings (attention) operate
near the noise floor and tolerate perturbation; the write channel into the
shared residual stream (FFN down-projection) is where errors compound
layer-over-layer; and boundary layers act as driven lattice nodes under
different dynamics.

These three independent lines of evidence---spectral concentration in keys,
directional concentration in query/key space, and tensor-role sensitivity in
compression---were developed without reference to each other or to the
coupled-oscillator framework, yet each converges on the same geometric picture
articulated in
Section~\ref{sec:mlp-modes}.

\subsection{Follow-On Directions}

Natural extensions of this work include:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Coherence-aware removal}: using the same anatomy scan to guide
SVD spectral filtering, rotation-compensated excision, and mean-compensated
pruning operators that respect the residual-stream geometry,
\item \textbf{Generalized BKT theory on $S^{d-1}$}: closing the full
critical-point theory for the vector Kuramoto / Lohe system,
\item \textbf{Coherence-during-training}: removing dead heads online during
training rather than post hoc.
\end{enumerate}
A companion open-source pipeline implementing the full coherence-guided
compression stack---head identification, channel pruning, and role-aware weight
quantization---is in preparation.

\subsection{Dead Heads Are Mildly Toxic}

A preliminary SVD rank sweep on the 30 dead heads identified in Gemma~3 4B
reveals that the optimal intervention is not deletion but spectral filtering.
At SVD rank $64$ ($25\%$ of the head dimension), the model \emph{improves}
by $\Delta\text{Loss}=-0.075$ ($-1.5\%$). This suggests dead heads are not
merely inert but are mild noise sources operating above the model's optimal
noise equilibrium. The full spectral filtering analysis and its integration
into a coherence-aware removal pipeline will be reported separately.

\section{Conclusion}

We derived and validated a zero-parameter dead-head identification law for frozen
transformers:
\begin{equation}
\boxed{\taudeath(d)=\frac{\chic}{\sqrt{d}}}, \qquad \chic \approx 0.96.
\end{equation}
The threshold comes from coupled-oscillator criticality and concentration of
measure, not from model-specific fitting. Across six decoder checkpoints in four
architecture families ($d=768$ through $d=4096$), the derived threshold achieves
$95.2\%$ through $100.0\%$ dead-head identification precision when validated
against individual head ablation ground truth.

The contribution is a geometric law: a physics-derived boundary that separates
coherently participating attention heads from dead ones, applicable to any frozen
transformer given only its hidden dimension. Convergent evidence from independent
work on spectral concentration, phase synchronization, and tensor-role sensitivity
in weight compression supports the underlying geometric picture. A companion
open-source pipeline for coherence-guided compression is in preparation.
